Insurance Forecasting with Regression Algorithms¶

In order to make money, insurance companies need to collect more in premiums than it spends on medical care to its beneficiaries. Every insurer makes a significant portion of its revenue by underwriting, which is basically charging a fee (called a premium) for taking on financial risk.

A great deal of time and money is invested to employ statistical and mathematical models to evaluate and mitigate the financial risk involved in different scenarios.

Therefore it is in the interest in the insurer to have data on insurance premiums and have models they can rely on to create the best contract.

The Data¶

The data comes from the book Machine Learning with R by Brett Lantz published by Packt Publishing. It is a simulated data set using hypothetical medical expenses. The data was gleamed from demographic statistics from the US Census Bureau for patients in the United States.

  • Since the data was already processed before being published, there are no missing values and all the data types are correct. Here is a quick rundown of each variable and their context:
  • age: An integer value indicating the age of the beneficiary.
  • sex: The policy holders gender.
  • bmi: The Body Mass index of the beneficiary. It's a measure of how overweight or underweight a person is relative to their height. The formula for BMI (English Formula) is equal to $\frac{Weight (lbs)}{[Height (in)]^2} * 703$
  • children: The number of children/dependents covered by the insurance plan.
  • smoker: A categorical variable that contains the classes "yes" or "no" that indicate whether the insured regularly smokes tabacco.
  • region: The beneficiary's place of residence within the United States divided into four geographic regions: northeast, southeast, southwest, or northwest.
  • expenses: The medical costs each person charged to the insurance plan for the year.
